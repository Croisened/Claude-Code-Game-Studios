# Robo Rhapsody — Neon Fugitive: Storyboard

**Date**: 2026-03-30
**Status**: Draft
**Author**: Nathanial Ryan + Claude Code

---

## Introduction

This storyboard captures the intended player experience of *Robo Rhapsody — Neon Fugitive* as a sequence of six cinematic beats — from first contact with Neotropolis to the moment a player's finger is already reaching for restart. Each panel describes a moment in the game's emotional arc, not a scripted cutscene. The panels are a visual and tonal brief for artists, animators, and anyone joining the project who needs to feel the game before playing it.

---

## Panel 1 — The City That Hunts

**Visual**: A low-angle, wide establishing shot looking up at a canyon of corporate towers. The camera sits at street level in the center lane of a gleaming chrome corridor — the run path, though we do not know it yet. The lane stretches deep into the Z axis, vanishing in neon haze. The flanking architecture is dense and vertical: illuminated corporate logos in ideographic script, surveillance cameras on every ledge, enforcement drones hovering at mid-height as dormant shapes in the fog. The lighting is cool blue and acid yellow — neon from above, grime from below. Rain streaks catch the light in long diagonals. The lane surface is wet chrome, reflecting everything. No robot in frame yet. The city breathes.

**Caption / On-screen text**: None. No UI. Pure world.

**Narrative beat**: Neotropolis is alive, hostile, and watching. The player understands the stakes before they see the protagonist.

**Transition**: The camera begins a slow zoom forward along the lane, as if something is about to enter the frame from behind.

---

## Panel 2 — This One Is Yours

**Visual**: Medium shot, three-quarter angle from slightly behind and to the left. The robot steps into frame and holds the center lane — confident, upright, briefly still. This is the idle stance. The robot is a humanoid mech: geometric torso, angular limbs, a head with a single wide-aperture optical sensor. Its texture set is vivid and specific — a particular palette, a particular pattern — unmistakably one robot out of ninety. The neon above backlights the robot's silhouette in a halo of violet and orange. In the foreground, soft focus: the lane surface reflecting the robot's colors back at the player. In the background, a wall of corporate surveillance infrastructure. The robot does not look at the camera. It looks forward.

**Caption / On-screen text**: Optional — a small UI watermark in the lower-right corner shows the token identifier: `RR #042`. Rendered in a monospace HUD font, low opacity. Subtle. It is there to be noticed, not announced.

**Narrative beat**: The robot is specific. It is not a generic avatar — it is *this* one, with this appearance, and the player recognizes it as theirs. The Identity First pillar lands.

**Transition**: A distant alarm tone — a city-wide alert. The robot's idle stance shifts. Its weight drops forward.

---

## Panel 3 — The First Stride

**Visual**: Dynamic close-up from a low, slightly forward-facing camera — the camera chases the robot from a low angle at knee height. The robot is mid-stride, run animation active: lean forward, arms pumping, optical sensor fixed on the path ahead. The environment rushes past in motion blur — chrome walls, neon signage, a surveillance camera whipping by. The lane tiles beneath the robot's feet scroll fast and continuous, no seam visible. Color temperature shifts: the cool blue of the establishing shot is warmer now — the robot's own texture colors dominate the frame. Speed lines from the flanking geometry. The environment is not passive backdrop; it streaks past the robot like it is fighting to keep up.

**Caption / On-screen text**: The HUD is live. Distance counter in the upper center: `0042 m`. Score in upper right, smaller. The font is clean and minimal — not decorative, not distracting. The values are ticking upward.

**Narrative beat**: The run has begun. The sensation of speed is immediate, physical, and joyful — this is the core fantasy delivered in motion.

**Transition**: The run counter rolls past 200m. In the upper distance, something new enters frame: a blocky shape hovering at lane center height.

---

## Panel 4 — The City Responds

**Visual**: Wide shot, over-the-shoulder from behind the robot. Three lanes visible. Ahead: an enforcement drone swoops down from the right flank, its scanner beam cutting a hot-white arc across the center lane. A second obstacle — a neon barrier, urgent red-orange — spans the left lane at waist height. The robot is between them, committed to a lane decision in the next fraction of a second. The environment is more chaotic now: holographic checkpoint banners triggering as the robot passes, strobing red alert lights from above, a collapsing section of flanking wall sending debris across the right lane. The feel is organized menace — the city is not randomly hostile, it is systematically closing. Everything is aimed at the robot. The robot's run animation leans harder into each stride.

**Caption / On-screen text**: Distance: `0847 m`. No prompts, no tutorial text. The scene communicates the decision entirely through geometry.

**Narrative beat**: The city is actively hunting. Every obstacle reads as a deliberate enforcement action, not random scenery. The player is solving a spatial problem at speed — and the city keeps escalating.

**Transition**: The drone clips the robot's left arm. Not a kill — a near-miss. The robot staggers one stride. The run continues. Then, three obstacles converge.

---

## Panel 5 — The City Catches Up

**Visual**: Tight over-the-shoulder medium shot, very close to the robot. Three obstacles arrive simultaneously: a drone low from the left, a barrier across center, a collapsing chunk of flanking architecture from the right — the robot has no lane to break to. Impact. The run animation cuts to the death animation: the robot's momentum carries it forward one extra stride before the legs go, a controlled collapse that still reads as fast and kinetic. The robot goes down in the center lane, slides a half-meter on its chest, and comes to rest. The neon of Neotropolis reflects in the wet lane surface around it. The city continues to scroll for two frames after the robot stops — the world keeps moving, indifferent. Silence except for ambient rain and distant city hum.

**Caption / On-screen text**: The HUD clears. No score visible yet. One brief moment of quiet — the robot and the city, no numbers between them.

**Narrative beat**: Death is cinematic and fast, not punishing. The robot went as far as it could. The city wins — this time.

**Transition**: A slow fade to the score screen. The robot's silhouette remains faintly visible behind the UI, held in the final frame of the death animation.

---

## Panel 6 — Run Again

**Visual**: The score screen. The robot's silhouette occupies the left half of the frame — ghosted, backlit by neon, still in the death pose but rendered at low opacity, becoming part of the background. The right half holds the score layout: `DISTANCE 0847 m` in large type, `BEST 1203 m` in a secondary line in cooler color, and below that, the robot's token identity again — `RR #042` — so the score belongs to a specific robot, not to a nameless run. A single button prompt at the bottom: `RUN AGAIN`. The button is already highlighted. The visual weight of the layout pulls the eye directly to it. The neon of Neotropolis bleeds in from the edges of the frame — the city is still there, still waiting. The robot's colors are still readable through the silhouette. It is not gone. It is ready.

**Caption / On-screen text**: `DISTANCE 0847 m` / `BEST 1203 m` / `RR #042` / `RUN AGAIN`. No failure language. No "Game Over." No retry count. The framing is entirely forward-looking.

**Narrative beat**: Death was not the end — it was a data point. The robot is right there. The city is right there. The gap between this run and the personal best is 356 meters, and that number is the only thing the player can think about.

**Transition**: The player presses `RUN AGAIN`. The score screen fades in under 300ms. The robot's idle animation snaps back to the start. The city is already moving.
